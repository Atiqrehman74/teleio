US B1/B2 Visa: Complete 2026 Application Guide

April 28, 2026 · 9 min read · By Teleio Travel Experts

B1 vs B2: Understanding the Difference

The US B1/B2 visa is the primary non-immigrant visa for visitors entering the United States for temporary, non-work purposes. While it is issued as a single combined visa, the two categories cover distinct travel purposes that are important to understand before you apply.

The B1 visa covers business-related visits: attending conferences or trade shows, negotiating contracts, consulting with associates, or settling an estate. Crucially, B1 holders may not receive a salary or payment from a US source during their stay.

The B2 visa covers tourism, recreation, and medical treatment. This includes holiday travel, visiting family or friends, participation in amateur events or competitions (where no payment is received), and receiving medical care at a US facility.

Most applicants apply for both simultaneously on a single form — the combined B1/B2 visa — giving them flexibility to conduct both personal and business activities during the same trip.

Who Needs a B1/B2 Visa?

Citizens of countries that participate in the US Visa Waiver Program (VWP) can enter the United States for up to 90 days without a visa, provided they have an approved ESTA (Electronic System for Travel Authorization). As of 2026, the VWP includes 42 countries, primarily from Western Europe, East Asia, and Oceania.

If your country is not on the VWP list — which includes most of South Asia, Africa, the Middle East, and Latin America — you are required to obtain a B1/B2 visa before travelling to the United States. Even VWP travellers who have been denied entry previously, or who plan to stay longer than 90 days, must apply for a visa.

Required Documents

Assembling a complete, well-organised document package is essential. For the B1/B2 visa, you will need:

  • Completed and submitted DS-160 nonimmigrant visa application form (online)
  • Valid passport with at least six months of validity beyond your intended US departure date
  • One recent passport-style photograph meeting US visa photo requirements (51mm x 51mm, white background)
  • MRV (Machine Readable Visa) fee payment receipt of USD $185
  • Bank statements for the past three to six months demonstrating sufficient funds to cover your US stay
  • Employment letter or proof of self-employment, including your salary and approved leave dates
  • Evidence of ties to your home country: property ownership, family documents, business ownership
  • Confirmed return flight booking (or itinerary showing your planned departure from the US)
  • Hotel reservations or a host invitation letter if staying with family or friends

Additional documents may be requested based on your personal circumstances — for example, medical records if you are travelling for treatment, or conference invitation letters for business travel.

DS-160 Form Guide

The DS-160 is the US government's online nonimmigrant visa application form. Every applicant must complete it at ceac.state.gov before scheduling an interview. The form collects detailed personal, travel, family, and background information and typically takes 45–90 minutes to complete.

Key tips for completing the DS-160:

  • Save your application ID and use the "Save" function regularly — the form times out after 20 minutes of inactivity.
  • Answer every question truthfully. The form includes questions about prior visa refusals, criminal history, and travel to certain countries. Dishonest answers can result in a permanent ban from the United States.
  • Upload a compliant digital photo directly in the form (the embassy may still require a physical print at interview).
  • Once submitted, you cannot edit the DS-160. Print and retain the confirmation page with your barcode — you will need this at your interview appointment.

Scheduling Your Interview

After completing the DS-160 and paying the MRV fee, you must schedule an in-person interview at the nearest US Embassy or Consulate in your country of residence. Interviews are mandatory for most applicants aged 14 to 79.

Wait times vary enormously by location and time of year. At popular consulates in countries such as India, Mexico, and Nigeria, applicants in 2026 are reporting waits of 300 to over 500 days for a standard tourist visa interview appointment. If your travel date is approaching, check whether an emergency or expedited appointment is available, though these are granted only for urgent, documented reasons.

To schedule your appointment, visit the US Embassy website for your country — each country operates through a separate appointment system. You will need your DS-160 confirmation number, MRV fee receipt number, and passport number to register.

Interview Preparation

The visa interview is typically brief — often five to ten minutes — but it carries enormous weight. The consular officer is assessing whether you qualify for a visa and whether you intend to return home after your US visit. Common questions include:

  • What is the purpose of your visit to the United States?
  • Where will you be staying and for how long?
  • Who is funding your trip?
  • What do you do for work, and who is your employer?
  • Do you have family in the United States?
  • Have you previously applied for or been denied a US visa?

Dress smartly (business casual is appropriate), arrive at least 30 minutes early, bring all original documents plus copies, and answer questions concisely and honestly. Do not bring mobile phones or electronic devices inside the embassy — most consulates prohibit them.

After the Interview

Most applicants receive an immediate decision at the interview window. If approved, your passport will be retained for visa printing and returned to you (typically by courier) within a few days to a couple of weeks.

If the officer issues a 221(g) notice, it means your case requires additional administrative processing or that you need to submit further documents. This can add days, weeks, or in some cases months to your wait. Track your application status at ceac.state.gov.

Note the 60-day rule: once your visa is issued, you must use it to enter the United States within 60 days of issue (or before your visa expires, whichever comes first). Your authorised period of stay, however, is determined by the CBP officer at the port of entry — typically up to six months.

Fees

The standard MRV application fee for a B1/B2 visa is USD $185, payable before your interview is scheduled. This fee is non-refundable, even if your visa is denied. Citizens of certain countries are also subject to a reciprocity fee, charged at the same rate that the applicant's country charges US citizens for equivalent visas. Check the US Department of State's reciprocity schedule for your nationality before budgeting.

Tips for First-Time Applicants

  • Apply as early as possible — given current interview wait times, 12–18 months before your intended travel date is not excessive in high-demand countries.
  • Be honest on every form and in every interview answer. Inconsistencies are grounds for immediate refusal and can complicate future applications.
  • Present a clear, specific itinerary rather than vague travel plans. Officers respond well to applicants who clearly know where they are going and why.
  • If you have previously held a US visa (even if expired), bring it to your interview — it demonstrates a history of compliance with US immigration rules.
  • Strong home ties — steady employment, family, property — are your most powerful tool. Make them visible in your document file.
